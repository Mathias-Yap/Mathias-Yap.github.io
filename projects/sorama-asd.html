Incorporating Spatial Acoustic Information for Anomalous Sound Detection

Background

Acoustic anomaly detection is used in industrial monitoring to identify abnormal machine behavior from sound. While modern acoustic monitoring systems often use microphone arrays, much of the existing research still focuses on single-channel audio. This leaves the value of spatial information, such as time differences between microphones or direction-related cues, relatively underexplored. This internship project investigated whether spatial features from a 64-channel microphone array could improve anomaly detection in an industrial setting at Sorama, where the existing approach relied on Isolation Forests and hand-engineered features.

Contribution

My contribution was the design and implementation of a modular acoustic anomaly detection framework inside Sorama’s internal AI development platform, ADAM. The framework formalizes anomaly detection as two separable components: a feature extractor and an anomaly scorer. This made it possible to train, evaluate, and compare different representations and scoring methods in a consistent pipeline. I also implemented autoencoder-based anomaly detection systems using mel spectrogram features, spatial GCC-PHAT features, and combined spectral-spatial representations. The framework supports sequential training, validation-based score normalization and thresholding, temporal windowing for variable-length audio, and deployment as a unified TensorFlow Keras model.

Results & Findings

The autoencoder-based systems outperformed Sorama’s existing Isolation Forest baseline on the evaluated industrial dataset. The strongest result came from a mel spectrogram autoencoder with decoder reconstruction error scoring, achieving a ROC–AUC of 0.826 compared with the Isolation Forest baseline of 0.735. GCC-PHAT spatial features also performed competitively with decoder reconstruction error, reaching a ROC–AUC of 0.821, but the combined mel and GCC-PHAT representation did not improve performance. This suggests that, under the evaluated conditions, learned spectral features were the most reliable signal for anomaly detection, while the spatial features provided limited additional benefit. The results also raised an important caveat: the performance of GCC-PHAT may have been partly caused by unintended spectral sensitivity introduced by the feature construction, rather than purely by spatial information.
