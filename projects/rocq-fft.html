Formalization of Fast Polynomial Multiplication in Rocq

Background

Formal verification uses mathematical proof techniques to verify that software and algorithms behave according to their specifications. This is especially important in safety-critical domains such as cybersecurity, healthcare, and transport, where subtle implementation errors can have serious consequences. This thesis focused on formalizing fast polynomial multiplication in Rocq (formerly Coq), a proof assistant used to define programs, specifications, and machine-checked proofs. The work was motivated by the Verified Calculus library, which supports formal verification for algorithms used in Ariadne. Since the library’s existing polynomial multiplication was implemented naively, this project explored whether a faster FFT-based multiplication algorithm could be formally specified and proven correct.

Contribution

My contribution was the formalization and verification of an FFT-based polynomial multiplication algorithm in Rocq. I defined dense polynomial representations, implemented core polynomial operations, and developed the formal machinery needed to reason about complex polynomial evaluation, roots of unity, the Discrete Fourier Transform, the Fast Fourier Transform, and their inverse transformations. A key part of the work was proving that the FFT computes the same result as the DFT, proving equivalence between the inverse FFT and inverse DFT, and showing that the final fast multiplication algorithm is equivalent to naive polynomial multiplication. The original proof was completed for real-coefficient polynomials with one assumed property concerning sums over complex roots of unity. After completion of my thesis, I proved this assumption correct too. The work was then adapted into the Ariadne library for floating-point arithmetic with rigorous error bounds, making it usable in model-checking workflows that require both efficiency and formal reliability.

Formalization in Rocq

Working in Rocq means turning mathematical ideas into definitions, specifications, and machine-checked proofs. For this project, I first had to formalize what a polynomial is, what it means to evaluate one, and how a polynomial can be decomposed into its even and odd coefficients. These definitions then become the foundation for proving the correctness of the FFT-based multiplication algorithm.

Representing polynomials

Definition dense_poly := list C.

A dense complex polynomial is represented as a list of complex coefficients.

Evaluating a polynomial

Fixpoint complex_eval'
  (d : nat) (pd : dense_poly) (c : C) : C :=
  match pd with
  | nil => 0
  | cd :: pSd =>
      cd * (c ^ d) + complex_eval' (S d) pSd c
  end.

Definition complex_eval (p : dense_poly) (c : C) : C :=
  complex_eval' 0 p c.

Notation "p [ x ]" := (complex_eval p x).

Evaluation is defined recursively over the coefficient list, while notation makes later statements closer to ordinary mathematical writing.

Proving FFT structure

Lemma even_and_odd : forall p x,
  \even_p [x ^ 2] + x * \odd_p [x ^ 2] = p [x].

This lemma proves that a polynomial can be reconstructed from its even and odd coefficient parts, which is the key decomposition used by the FFT.

This example captures the kind of work involved in the formalization. Before proving the final multiplication algorithm correct, I had to express basic mathematical concepts in Rocq and prove that each transformation preserves the meaning of the polynomial. The final FFT proof depends on many such intermediate results, each checked by the proof assistant.

Results & Findings

The thesis showed that it is possible to implement and prove the correctness of a fast polynomial multiplication algorithm in Rocq. The formalized algorithm uses the FFT to move polynomials from coefficient representation to point-value representation, performs pointwise multiplication, and then applies the inverse FFT to return to coefficient form. This reduces the intended algorithmic complexity from the quadratic complexity of naive multiplication to O(n log n).

Retrospective

This project taught me a lot about rigorous and formal mathematics, which is still one of the parts of computer science I enjoy most. Working in Coq forced me to be precise about every definition, recursive structure, and proof obligation. Even concepts that feel straightforward on paper, such as polynomial evaluation or the FFT butterfly step, required careful formalization before they could be used reliably. In hindsight, the final assumption that made it into the paper was easily provable: that the relevant roots of unity sum to zero. At the time, I expected that proving this would require a more general proof of the geometric series, which felt outside the scope of the project. Shortly after submission, I realized that for the case needed in my formalization, a simpler argument was enough. Since the algorithm only required roots of unity of size 2n, the roots could be paired with their opposites on the unit circle, and each pair sums to zero. The original motivation was to adapt this proof into the Ariadne library, where polynomial multiplication is used in model-checking workflows. That would require extending the result to floating-point arithmetic with rigorous error bounds. While the FFT’s structure makes this relatively manageable, since it mainly consists of additions and multiplications by known constants, the floating-point version still needs to track rounding error, approximated roots of unity, and accumulated error through the transform.
